Running Shoe Buying Guide

Choosing the right running shoe depends on your goals, your foot mechanics, and where you run. Use this guide to find the best match for your situation.

Best running shoes for beginners

If you are new to running, your top priority should be cushioning and stability, not speed. Beginner runners tend to have an inconsistent gait and are at higher risk of overuse injuries if they start in a shoe that is too minimal or too rigid.

  • Look for a heel-to-toe drop between 8mm and 12mm to ease the transition into running.
  • Choose a shoe with a wide base for balance and confidence on the road.
  • Avoid racing flats or carbon-plated shoes until you have at least six months of consistent running behind you.
  • Our pick: Stride EasyRun — generous cushioning, forgiving fit, beginner-friendly price point.

How to choose running shoes

The most important factors when choosing a running shoe are: your foot type (neutral, overpronating, or supinating), the terrain you run on, your weekly mileage, and any existing injuries or physical conditions.

  • Foot type: Wet your foot and step on paper to see your arch shape. A full footprint suggests flat feet and likely overpronation; a narrow midband suggests a high arch and possible supination.
  • Terrain: Road running needs cushioned, smooth outsoles. Trail running needs lugged, grippy soles with protective overlays.
  • Mileage: High-mileage runners need more durable, cushioned shoes. Shoes typically last 500–800km before the midsole breaks down.
  • Fit: Always leave a thumb's width between your longest toe and the end of the shoe. Your heel should feel locked in with no slipping.

Running shoes for mobility issues

Runners with limited mobility in their hips, knees, or ankles require shoes that compensate for reduced range of motion and protect vulnerable joints from repetitive stress. Key features to look for include:

  • Rocker sole geometry: Assists the foot through its natural gait cycle even when ankle flexion is limited.
  • Zero or low drop: Reduces the angle at which the foot strikes the ground, lowering strain on the knee and hip.
  • Extra-wide fit: Prevents compression of the forefoot, which is especially important for runners with arthritis or post-surgical swelling.
  • Our pick: Stride MobilityFlex — designed in consultation with physiotherapists for exactly this runner profile.

Running shoes for weak ankles

Ankle instability is one of the most common issues among recreational runners. It can stem from previous sprains, hypermobility, or underdeveloped stabilising muscles. The right shoe provides structural support while you build strength.

  • High-cut collar: Wraps around the ankle to limit excessive lateral movement.
  • Reinforced heel counter: A firm, structured heel cup controls rearfoot motion and prevents the ankle rolling outward.
  • Dual-density midsole: Firmer foam on the medial (inner) side resists collapse during ground contact.
  • Pair your shoe with ankle-strengthening exercises — single-leg calf raises, balance board training, and resistance band rotations.
  • Our pick: Stride AnkleGuard — the highest level of ankle support in our range.

Running shoes for trail runs

Trail running demands a completely different shoe from road running. The terrain is unpredictable, the surface is soft and uneven, and your foot needs protection from sharp objects underfoot.

  • Lugged outsole: Deep, multidirectional lugs dig into soft ground and eject mud for consistent traction.
  • Rock plate: A stiff nylon or TPU plate sits between the midsole and outsole to protect against sharp stones and roots.
  • Protective toe cap: Reinforced rubber at the front guards against stubbed toes on rocky terrain.
  • Fit: Go half a size up for trails — feet swell on longer runs and you need room to move when descending steep slopes.
  • Our pick: Stride TrailMaster — built for technical terrain with waterproofing for year-round trail use.

Running shoes for a first marathon

Training for and completing your first marathon is a significant physical undertaking. Your shoe needs to perform reliably over months of training runs and then hold up for the race itself — typically 4 to 6 hours on your feet for a first-timer.

  • Cushioning: Maximum cushioning is non-negotiable for first-time marathoners. Joints accumulate significant fatigue after kilometre 30 — a well-cushioned shoe delays the onset of that pain.
  • Energy return: A carbon plate or highly responsive foam (like PEBA-based foams) returns energy with each stride, helping you maintain pace in the final 10km when fatigue sets in.
  • Break-in period: Never race in a new shoe. Log at least 80–100km in your marathon shoe before race day to avoid blisters and hot spots.
  • Weight: A lighter shoe reduces the cumulative effort of 42,000+ foot strikes. Under 280g per shoe is a good target for race day.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the difference between road and trail running shoes?

Road running shoes are designed for hard, predictable surfaces like asphalt and concrete. They prioritise cushioning and a smooth ride. Trail running shoes are built for soft, uneven terrain — they have aggressive outsole lugs for grip, rock plates for underfoot protection, and reinforced uppers to handle debris. Using a road shoe on trails significantly increases the risk of slipping and foot injury.

Can I run a marathon in the same shoes I trained in?

You should train in your marathon shoes, but ideally you rotate between two pairs. A shoe used for all your training runs will be significantly worn down by race day, losing both cushioning and structural integrity. Many experienced runners keep a "fresher" pair with around 100km on them specifically for race day. A completely new pair on race day is a risk — blisters and hot spots from unbroken shoes are a common cause of DNFs.

How often should I replace my running shoes?

Most running shoes last between 500 and 800 kilometres before the midsole foam degrades enough to reduce its protective properties. Signs of wear include: the heel counter collapsing inward, visible compression lines in the midsole foam, outsole rubber worn smooth, or a noticeable increase in leg fatigue after runs. Heavier runners or those who run on concrete may need to replace shoes closer to the 500km mark.

Are running shoes suitable for people with flat feet?

Yes, but flat-footed runners should look specifically for stability or motion-control shoes. Flat feet typically correlate with overpronation — an inward rolling of the foot on landing — which, if left unsupported, can lead to shin splints, plantar fasciitis, and knee pain. Stability shoes have a denser foam wedge on the medial side to resist excessive inward roll. In more severe cases, custom orthotics combined with a neutral shoe may be more effective than an off-the-shelf stability shoe.

What running shoes are best for someone with weak ankles?

Runners with weak or unstable ankles benefit most from shoes with a high-cut ankle collar, a firm heel counter, and a wide, stable base. The Stride AnkleGuard was designed specifically for this profile. It is also worth combining supportive footwear with a targeted ankle-strengthening programme — the shoe manages instability in the short term, but building the muscles around the ankle joint is the long-term solution.

Can running help people with mobility issues?

Running can be beneficial for people with certain mobility issues, particularly when started gradually and with the right footwear and professional guidance. Low-impact jogging can improve joint lubrication, strengthen stabilising muscles, and support weight management, all of which contribute positively to mobility. However, runners with mobility limitations should consult a physiotherapist before starting a programme, and choose footwear specifically designed to reduce joint stress — such as the Stride MobilityFlex.

What is heel-to-toe drop in a running shoe?

Heel-to-toe drop (also called heel drop or offset) is the difference in height between the heel and the forefoot of the shoe. A higher drop (10–12mm) places the heel lower relative to the toe, which suits heel-strikers and runners transitioning from casual footwear. A lower drop (0–4mm) encourages a midfoot or forefoot strike, which can reduce knee stress but places more load on the Achilles tendon and calf. Beginners are generally advised to start with a higher drop and reduce it gradually if they wish to transition.

Do I need different shoes for training and race day?

For marathon runners, yes — many coaches recommend a "workhorse" training shoe for the bulk of your mileage and a lighter, more responsive race shoe for race day. The training shoe absorbs the repetitive stress of long runs and tempo sessions, preserving the race shoe's foam and energy-return properties for when they matter most. That said, it is essential to log enough kilometres in the race shoe to be comfortable in it before the event.
